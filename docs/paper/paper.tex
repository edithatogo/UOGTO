\begin{abstract}
Game theory studies choices between people, groups, or computer agents when each choice can affect the others. These models are now used in economics, public policy, safety, medicine, and artificial intelligence (AI). But the parts of a game are often described in different ways. One paper may use prose. Another may use code. Another may use a diagram. That makes it hard to compare the work. We present the \abblink{uogto}{Universal Open Game Theory Ontology (UOGTO)}. An ontology is a shared dictionary that computers and people can inspect. UOGTO gives names to players, actions, \glslink{strategy}{strategies}, information, \glslink{payoff}{payoffs}, \glslink{mechanism}{mechanisms}, \glslink{outcome}{outcomes}, \glslink{session}{sessions}, and \glslink{trace}{traces}. It separates the plan for a game from one actual run of that game. It also records where each claim came from. We compared 21 source families. We normalised 4,046 term rows, reviewed 460 possible mappings, and accepted 12 careful alignments. We kept rejected rows as \glslink{negative-evidence}{negative evidence}. UOGTO is not a complete list of all game theory. It is also not a theory of human behaviour. Its role is practical: it helps readers see what a game model assumes, what evidence supports it, and what still needs review.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Plain-language summary}
This paper is about making game models easier to check. A game model can describe an auction, a public-goods problem, a safety decision, or an AI agent choosing tools. To understand such a model, a reader needs to know five basic things: who can act, what they can do, what they know, what they gain or lose, and what happened.

UOGTO is a shared vocabulary for those things. It works like a careful label system. It says, for example, that the rules of an auction are not the same thing as one auction that happened on Tuesday. It also says that a guessed mapping between two words is not the same as an accepted mapping. This matters because similar words can mean different things in different fields.

The main result is not that UOGTO covers every game. It does not. The result is a checked way to build and review a general game-theory vocabulary. The repository keeps the search records, rejected options, diagrams, tables, and validation checks so that readers can inspect the work.

\section{Introduction}
Game theory gives us a way to talk about strategic choice. It includes classic ideas such as equilibrium, auctions, matching, and the Prisoner's Dilemma \cite{nash-1950,harsanyi-1967,shapley-1953,vickrey-1961,myerson-1981,gale-shapley-1962,rapoport-chammah-1965}. It also includes modern computer settings, such as reinforcement-learning game environments \cite{nisan-2007,open-spiel-2019,arxiv-2006-06580v3}. These ideas appear in many fields. They appear in economics, safety, public policy, medical decisions, simulations, and AI agents.

The problem is that the same game idea can be written in many forms. It may be a payoff matrix, a Python program, a trace from a simulation, a standards document, or a graph. Each form can be useful. But they often hide important assumptions. A reader may not know who the players are, what actions are allowed, what each player knows, what payoff is used, or what evidence supports the result.

This is more than a style problem. If the assumptions are hidden, people cannot compare studies well. They also cannot check whether an AI experiment, policy model, safety case, or medical decision model is using the same idea as another study. A model may run as code and still be hard to understand.

UOGTO addresses this gap by using linked data. Linked data is a way to write facts as a graph that computers can inspect. UOGTO uses \abblink{rdf}{Resource Description Framework (RDF)}, \abblink{owl}{Web Ontology Language (OWL)}, \abblink{shacl}{Shapes Constraint Language (SHACL)}, \abblink{jsonld}{JavaScript Object Notation for Linked Data (JSON-LD)}, and \abblink{sparql}{SPARQL} \cite{gruber-1995,w3c-rdf11-concepts,w3c-owl2-overview,w3c-shacl,w3c-json-ld11,w3c-sparql11-query}. These are standard tools for writing and checking knowledge graphs. The goal is not to replace maths or game theory. The goal is to make game models easier to inspect, cite, reuse, and test.

The project started with a broad search. We did not begin with one field's favourite examples. That choice matters. A field may often use auctions or the Prisoner's Dilemma because those examples are useful for that field. But that does not mean other game forms are irrelevant. UOGTO records candidate games and features first. It then decides whether each idea belongs in the core ontology, an extension, an external mapping, a review queue, or a rejection record. This makes \glslink{disciplinary-selection-bias}{disciplinary selection bias} easier to see.

This paper evaluates UOGTO by its design choices and by the evidence records behind those choices. The repository stores source records, mapping review data, \abblink{sssom}{SSSOM} mapping tables, \abblink{shacl}{SHACL} examples, quality metrics, and decision ledgers. The paper limits its claims when the evidence is only documentary or metadata-only. Table~\ref{tab:evidence-surfaces} summarises the main evidence surfaces.

For a behavioural-science audience, UOGTO is best understood as a resource. It is not a primary empirical result. It does not test a theory of human behaviour. It gives shared terms for asking simple questions about a study. Who acted? What choices were available? What incentives operated? What outcome was observed? What evidence supports that claim?

\section{Results}
\subsection{Source discovery found a broad but uneven evidence base}
The search produced two linked sets of results. The first set was a source register. It records what we searched, what we kept, and what we did not find. This was a scoping workflow, not a full systematic review with two independent screeners. It found six routes that added sources and one route that found no extra source. In total, 39 source records were kept.

The second set was the comparison package. It used a narrower group of 21 sources across 17 source families. These sources were relevant to game theory, simulation, agent-based models, system dynamics, provenance, web schemas, workflows, and related formalisms. Together, they produced 3,485 external term rows. UOGTO contributed 561 term rows. That gave 4,046 term rows for comparison.

The evidence was uneven. Some sources were parsed \abblink{rdf}{RDF}. Some were structured standards or schemas. Some were metadata-only records. Some were literature records. This matters because a parsed ontology file supports stronger claims than a web page about a standard. The workflow made that difference visible.

The mapping review was careful by design. It reviewed 460 possible mappings. It accepted 12. It kept 448 as rejected or not asserted. These rejected rows are not waste. They are \glslink{negative-evidence}{negative evidence}. They show what was considered and why it was not claimed. Figure~\ref{fig:systematic-search-prisma-flow} shows the process.

\subsection{A semantic core separates games, executions, and evidence}
UOGTO's main design choice is simple. It keeps the plan for a game separate from one run of that game. A \glslink{game-specification}{game specification} describes the rules and possible choices. A \glslink{game-instance}{game instance} fills in concrete details. A \glslink{session}{session} records one run. A \glslink{trace}{trace} records evidence from that run.

This separation avoids confusion. The word ``game'' can mean several things. It can mean a general form, such as a Prisoner's Dilemma. It can mean a configured simulation. It can also mean one recorded play-through. UOGTO gives each meaning its own place.

The examples cover normal-form games, extensive-form games, cooperative games, auctions, matching markets, voting, Markov games, security games, privacy-budget games, large language model (LLM) tool-use games, Petri-net execution games, and digital-twin security games. These examples are useful checks. But they do not prove that every extension is complete. Some economics-facing modules still need richer example graphs before they can support strong claims.

A first-price auction shows the pattern. In this auction, bidders each submit one sealed bid. The highest bidder wins and pays the amount they bid. UOGTO records the auction rules as the game specification. It records named bidders and values as an instance. It records the submitted bids and final payment as a session and trace. This lets the same auction support economic analysis, simulation, and audit without mixing those layers.

\subsection{Behavioural and policy use cases}
UOGTO is useful when a study depends on strategic choices and the assumptions need to be checked. In a public-goods problem, it can record the people involved, the choices to contribute or not contribute, the information each person has, the rules, the outcomes, and the trace of what happened. In an AI setting, it can record human agents, AI agents, tool-use actions, policies, observations, and provenance. In a safety or medical decision setting, it can record actors, choices, incentives, risks, and outcomes.

These examples do not mean UOGTO proves a policy is safe or a treatment is useful. It does not. It helps describe the strategic part of the evidence in a way that can be checked.

\subsection{Comparative mapping favours precision over broad equivalence}
The comparison workflow reviewed 21 source families. It produced 4,046 term rows and 460 possible mappings. It accepted 12 mappings and rejected 448. This low acceptance rate is deliberate. Similar words are not enough. Two fields can use the same word for different ideas, or different words for the same idea.

\glslink{accepted-mapping}{Accepted alignments} are published in Turtle and \abblink{sssom}{SSSOM}. Rejected and review-needed rows stay in the data. This is important. A reader can see both what UOGTO claims and what it does not claim.

\subsection{Network analyses show bridges, not a universal merger}
The network analysis asks a simple question: which sources and modules appear close to each other in the evidence? It found 74 nodes in the import/evidence graph, 69 nodes and 391 edges in the source-similarity graph, 12 term-alignment edges, and 11 module-coverage edges. A node is a source, module, term, or evidence group. An edge is a link between two nodes.

The graphs show bridges. They do not prove that all fields mean the same thing. Mean-field games, cooperative games, network games, congestion-routing games, evolutionary games, compositional open games, epistemic games, and causal games were central in the current evidence graph. That means they are useful review priorities. It does not mean they are finished modules.

The analysis used three views. The source-similarity graph shows overlap among sources and UOGTO modules. The term-alignment graph shows accepted links between UOGTO terms and external terms. The import/evidence-use graph separates parsed sources from metadata-only or documentary evidence. These views help readers see where the evidence is strong and where it is thin.

The repository includes static \abblink{svg}{SVG} images, PDF copies for the arXiv appendix, and Cosmograph-ready node and edge files. Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:source-similarity-cosmograph}--\ref{fig:import-evidence-use-cosmograph} show the three main graph views.

\subsection{Packaging for reuse and audit}
UOGTO is packaged as more than one vocabulary file. The release includes citation metadata, registry records, documentation, \abblink{rocrate}{RO-Crate} metadata, tabular artefacts, and \abblink{ci}{continuous integration (CI)} validation. It also includes source discovery records, negative evidence, review samples, case studies, and decision ledgers.

This packaging matters. UOGTO is both a proposal and a testable research object. Readers can inspect terms, rerun checks, review mapping decisions, and trace claims back to files.

\section{Discussion}
UOGTO responds to a practical problem. Game models now move across fields faster than their assumptions can be checked. A shared ontology cannot choose the right solution for a game. It cannot prove that a simulation is realistic. It cannot decide whether a mechanism is fair. It can make the parts of the model visible. That helps readers ask better questions about players, choices, information, payoffs, mechanisms, traces, and outcomes.

The most important design choice is restraint. UOGTO keeps a \glslink{game-specification}{game specification} separate from a recorded run. It also keeps a \glslink{mapping-candidate}{mapping candidate} separate from an \glslink{accepted-mapping}{accepted mapping}. This matters because similar words do not always mean the same thing. A low number of accepted mappings is therefore not a weakness. It shows that the project avoids claiming more than the evidence supports.

The second contribution is the workflow. UOGTO is not only a term list. It links ontology modules, examples, shapes, test questions, mapping decisions, source records, quality checks, and network figures. A reviewer can inspect each layer. They can rerun validation, check rejected mappings, challenge source decisions, or reload the graph exports.

The search and graph results also show why a broad ontology is needed. Each field tends to reuse the game examples that fit its own problems. Economists may focus on auctions or public-goods games. Safety researchers may focus on reporting, compliance, and coordination. Health economists may focus on incentives, resource allocation, reimbursement, uptake, and outcome attribution. Those examples are useful, but they should not define the full boundary of game theory. UOGTO makes this boundary visible by recording what was included, rejected, deferred, or sent for domain review.

The network figures support the same point. Some bridge nodes are real topic areas, such as mean-field, cooperative, network, congestion-routing, evolutionary, compositional, epistemic, and causal games. Other bridges are broad metadata terms. A bridge in a graph means "review this connection". It does not mean "these fields are the same".

The claim is therefore moderate. UOGTO is not a complete catalogue of game theory. It is a repeatable way to build a shared layer from uneven evidence. The pattern is simple: search broadly, extract features, separate strong evidence from weak evidence, accept mappings carefully, and keep negative evidence visible. This is useful for executable research because runnable code can still hide important assumptions.

Future work should add concepts selectively. A term should enter UOGTO when it improves reusable game meaning. Otherwise it should be aligned externally, deferred, rejected as a duplicate, rejected as out of scope, or sent to domain review. This keeps the ontology general. It also lets health economics, medical decision modelling, safety systems, and genomic-policy work add their own outcomes, risks, constraints, and evidence standards without changing the shared game layer.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Ontology representation and validation}
UOGTO is written in \abblink{rdf}{RDF} and \abblink{owl}{OWL 2} \cite{w3c-rdf11-concepts,w3c-owl2-overview}. RDF stores facts as links. OWL adds formal meaning. \abblink{jsonld}{JSON-LD} gives web-friendly files. \abblink{shacl}{SHACL} checks that examples follow expected patterns \cite{w3c-json-ld11,w3c-shacl}. \abblink{sparql}{SPARQL} is used for test questions \cite{w3c-sparql11-query}. The checks parse the ontology. They check examples. They run 10 test questions. These questions ask about players, strategies, equilibria, hidden information, security targets, incentives, language-model tool calls, traces, privacy budgets, and module imports. The portable checks use \abblink{rdflib}{RDFLib} and \abblink{pyshacl}{pySHACL}. Extra reports are kept when Java tools are available.

\subsection{Ontology construction workflow}
UOGTO was built in four steps. First, the project searched for game forms, game-theory rules, run-time settings, simulation terms, related ontologies, and standards. Second, it extracted candidate features. These included participants, actions, strategies, information, payoffs, outcomes, mechanisms, timing, traces, provenance, decision models, resource rules, incentives, and outcome pathways. Third, reusable concepts were assigned to the core ontology, extension modules, JSON-LD files, SHACL shapes, SPARQL questions, or external mappings. Fourth, concepts that were not ready were still recorded as missing, rejected, deferred, or needing domain review.

This workflow treats UOGTO as a bridge over mixed evidence. A game form can enter the workflow because it appears in formal game theory, a simulator, a security paper, an economics paper, a standard, or a repository. Entry into the workflow does not mean automatic entry into the ontology core. It creates a decision point that can be checked later.

\subsection{Source discovery and screening}
Source discovery used a \abblink{prismascr}{PRISMA-ScR}-style scoping protocol over a June 2026 repository snapshot. Searches used OpenAlex, arXiv, Crossref, public ontology registries, standards documents, and repository search \cite{openalex,arxiv-api,crossref}. Each search event records the query, result count, evidence level, inclusion reason, licence status, and reviewer handoff. If two searches found the same source, they updated one row.

Sources were included when they gave usable terms for games, agents, simulation, execution, provenance, observation, time, or systems modelling. They could be an ontology, vocabulary, schema, metamodel, standard, upper ontology, or stable repository surface. Sources were excluded or deferred when they were prose only, lacked a term list, lacked a stable vocabulary surface, had little game or execution overlap, could not be shared safely, or could not be audited. This was a structured scoping process. It was not a full dual-screened systematic review. Health and outcomes relevance is captured through game theory, simulation, ontology, standards, and policy-modelling routes. It is not based on a dedicated health technology assessment review.

\subsection{Mapping review and robustness}
The mapping pipeline first normalizes terms. It then proposes candidate links. The signals include labels, definitions, hierarchy, property use, type fit, and source quality. A candidate link is not an accepted link. Accepted mappings are careful claims that can be reviewed. They are exported in Turtle and \abblink{sssom}{SSSOM}. Robustness checks remove one signal at a time. This shows whether a result depends too much on one signal. Empty review fields are treated as evidence gaps, not as agreement.

\subsection{Graph and network analysis}
Graph analyses use the term list, source records, accepted mapping rows, and module-coverage tables. A \glslink{node}{node} is a source, module, term, or evidence group. An \glslink{edge}{edge} is a link between two nodes. The source-similarity graph links sources or modules when their terms, imports, formats, or coverage overlap. The term-alignment graph links accepted UOGTO terms to external terms. The import/evidence-use graph separates parsed ontology files from metadata-only evidence groups. Edge weights record overlap or accepted mappings. Node degree is used as a simple centrality score. The analysis helps identify review priorities. It does not prove that the ontology is correct.

\subsection{Repository and manuscript source integrity}
References are stored in \abblink{csl}{CSL} \abblink{json}{JSON} and matched to LaTeX citation keys. Repository checks inspect the source package for hidden files, private notes, auxiliary files, unused figures, embedded metadata, credentials, and private URLs. These checks improve source hygiene. They do not mean that every cited source endorses UOGTO.

\section{Conclusion}
UOGTO provides a shared layer for game-theory research. It was built from a broad search, feature extraction, careful mapping review, validation, and graph analysis. It is not a final catalogue of all game theory. It is also not a theory of behaviour. It is a repeatable way to describe games, sessions, traces, strategies, actions, payoffs, outcomes, mechanisms, mappings, and evidence.

The main contribution is that claims can be checked. Domain-specific vocabularies and familiar examples remain useful. They should not set the limits of a general ontology. UOGTO keeps accepted, rejected, deferred, metadata-only, and domain-review evidence visible. This makes future extension work easier to check. It also makes computational game-theory evidence easier to validate, compare, cite, and reuse in fields where decisions, incentives, risks, and outcomes are linked.

\section{Limitations}
UOGTO has four main limits. First, metadata-only sources cannot support the same claims as parsed RDF files. Second, accepted mappings are sparse by design. This improves precision, but it leaves many plausible links for future review. Third, the search is not exhaustive. Search coverage, English-language bias, repository visibility, standards access, licence limits, snapshot timing, and the lack of dedicated behavioural-science, safety-science, health technology assessment, or outcomes searches all limit coverage. Fourth, UOGTO is broad but incomplete. New game-theory concepts should pass through the disposition workflow before they are added.

\section*{Glossary}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Glossary}

\begin{description}
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:accepted-mapping}{Accepted mapping}] A reviewed alignment that UOGTO publishes as an asserted correspondence rather than merely a candidate.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:action}{Action}] A move, operation, or choice available to a player or agent within a game description or execution.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:alignment}{Alignment}] A recorded relationship between a UOGTO term and an external term, ranging from a candidate correspondence to an accepted mapping.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:competency-question}{Competency question}] A testable question that an ontology and its examples should be able to answer, usually through a query.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:datatype-property}{Datatype property}] An OWL property that links an ontology individual to a literal value such as a string, number, date, or boolean.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:disciplinary-selection-bias}{Disciplinary selection bias}] The tendency for a field to model the game forms most salient to its own problems while omitting other relevant forms without making that boundary explicit.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:domain-specific-ontology}{Domain-specific ontology}] An ontology or vocabulary optimized for one disciplinary, application, or standards context rather than for general game-theoretic reuse.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:edge}{Edge}] A graph link between two nodes, such as a link between a source and a UOGTO module.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:feature-extraction}{Feature extraction}] The structured recording of game features such as participants, actions, strategies, information assumptions, payoffs, outcomes, mechanisms, execution semantics, and provenance requirements.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:first-price-auction}{First-price auction}] An auction where bidders submit sealed bids and the highest bidder wins and pays their own bid.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:game-instance}{Game instance}] A configured occurrence of a game specification, potentially with concrete players, parameters, or initial conditions.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:game-specification}{Game specification}] The abstract structural description of a game, separated from any particular execution or recorded run.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:knowledge-graph}{Knowledge graph}] A linked data structure that represents things and their relationships in a form that software can query.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:linked-data}{Linked data}] A way of publishing structured facts so that identifiers and relationships can connect across datasets.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:mapping-candidate}{Mapping candidate}] A possible correspondence between UOGTO and an external term that still requires review before it can be asserted.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:mechanism}{Mechanism}] A rule, institution, protocol, or design surface that structures incentives and outcomes in a game.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:metadata-only-source}{Metadata-only source}] A source represented through bibliographic or descriptive metadata rather than parsed ontology or structured domain content.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:module-audit}{Module audit}] A generated inspection table recording module-level annotations, validation links, examples, competency questions, and reasoner status.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:negative-evidence}{Negative evidence}] Retained evidence of rejected, deferred, or non-asserted mappings and concepts, used to show what was considered but not claimed.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:node}{Node}] A graph item, such as a source, module, term, or evidence group.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:object-property}{Object property}] An OWL property that links one ontology individual to another individual.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:ontology}{Ontology}] A structured vocabulary that defines the main types of things in a domain and how they relate to each other.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:ontology-module}{Ontology module}] A separately maintained ontology file or component that contributes a coherent part of the UOGTO vocabulary.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:outcome}{Outcome}] A terminal or intermediate result of a game, mechanism, session, or trace.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:payoff}{Payoff}] A value, utility, cost, reward, or preference-bearing quantity assigned to an agent, action, state, or outcome.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:session}{Session}] A recorded occurrence or run of a game, distinct from the abstract game specification.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:shape}{Shape}] A SHACL constraint definition used to validate selected closed-world expectations over RDF data.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:strategy}{Strategy}] A contingent plan, policy, or rule for choosing actions in response to game states or information.
\item[\hypertarget{gloss:trace}{Trace}] Evidence produced by an execution, such as events, actions, observations, state transitions, and provenance records.
\end{description}
